# Write and Share: Your Personal Brand on LinkedIn

:::info Your Code Alone Won't Get You Hired

In 2026, 87% of recruiters use LinkedIn to find candidates. Your GitHub profile shows what you built. Your LinkedIn profile shows how you think about it. Both matter.
:::

## Why Writing Matters

Writing about your work accomplishes three things:

1. **Demonstrates communication skills** — Engineers who can explain complex topics are rare and valuable
2. **Creates discoverability** — Recruiters search for keywords in posts and articles
3. **Builds authority** — You become known as an expert in your niche

## The LinkedIn Content Strategy

### What to Post (3x per week)

| Day | Content Type | Example |
|-----|-------------|---------|
| Monday | Build update | "This week I'm building an AI agent that processes invoices automatically. Here's the architecture..." |
| Wednesday | Learning insight | "TIL: LangGraph's graph-based state is fundamentally different from CrewAI's role-based approach. Here's why it matters..." |
| Friday | Hot take / opinion | "Controversial opinion: Most developers don't need to learn 10 frameworks. They need to learn how to think about systems." |

### What to Write About

- **Your projects** — What you're building, why, and what you learned
- **Your failures** — "I deployed a bug to production. Here's what happened." (These posts go viral)
- **Your opinions** — What's overhyped, what's underrated, what's changing
- **Your learning** — New tools, frameworks, or concepts you're mastering
- **Your open source work** — Contributions, PRs, and what you learned from the code review

## The Post Framework

Every post should follow this structure:

```
Hook: One sentence that makes people stop scrolling
Body: The actual content (2-3 short paragraphs max)
CTA: What should the reader do next?
```

### Example Post:

```
"I just got rejected from 200 jobs. Then I deployed one project and got 5 offers.

Here's what changed:

After 200 rejections, I stopped applying. Instead, I built a RAG system that 
searches my company's internal docs. I deployed it. Shared it on LinkedIn.

Three weeks later, a recruiter from [Company] messaged me: 'I saw your RAG 
project. We're building something similar. Want to chat?'

The lesson: Stop sending resumes. Start showing your work.

What's one project you could deploy this week?"
```

## Content That Gets Traction

### High Engagement Posts
- "I built X in Y hours using AI"
- "My biggest mistake as a developer"
- "What I learned from [famous engineer's] code"
- "Here's the tech stack behind [your project]"
- "Stop doing X. Do Y instead."

### Low Engagement Posts
- Generic motivational quotes
- "Excited to announce..." (nobody cares unless it's specific)
- Vague career advice without personal experience
- Long technical tutorials (save those for articles, not posts)

## The Writing Schedule

| Activity | Frequency | Time |
|---------|-----------|------|
| LinkedIn post | 3x/week | 15 min per post |
| Blog/article | 1x/month | 2-3 hours |
| Twitter thread | 2x/week | 10 min each |
| Open source write-up | 1x/month | 1-2 hours |

## The Compound Effect

```
Month 1: 3 posts/week → 50 followers, 20 likes/post
Month 3: 3 posts/week → 200 followers, 50 likes/post
Month 6: 3 posts/week → 500 followers, 100 likes/post, first recruiter message
Month 12: 3 posts/week → 2,000+ followers, inbound job offers
```

:::warning The Writing Rule

**Consistency beats perfection.** A mediocre post 3x/week beats a perfect article once a month. Your audience needs to see your name regularly to remember you.

## Checklist

- [ ] Update your LinkedIn profile with a clear headline and summary
- [ ] Write your first LinkedIn post about a project you're working on
- [ ] Share your first open source contribution
- [ ] Set a schedule: 3 posts/week minimum
- [ ] Engage with other developers' posts (comment thoughtfully)
- [ ] Track what gets engagement and do more of it
