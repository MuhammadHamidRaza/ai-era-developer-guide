# Get Referrals and Build Your Network

:::info The Hidden Job Market

80% of jobs are never posted publicly. They're filled through referrals, internal recommendations, and networks. If you're only applying to posted jobs, you're competing for the remaining 20%.
:::

## Why Referrals Beat Applications

| Method | Response Rate | Time to Hire | Competition |
|--------|--------------|-------------|-------------|
| Cold application | 1-3% | 4-8 weeks | 500+ applicants |
| Referral from employee | 30-50% | 1-3 weeks | 5-10 applicants |
| Direct recruiter outreach | 40-60% | 1-2 weeks | 3-5 applicants |

A referral is worth 100 cold applications.

## How to Get Referrals

### Strategy 1: Build in Public

When you deploy projects and share them on LinkedIn, people notice. The referral comes naturally:

> "Hey, I saw your RAG project. My company is building something similar. Want me to refer you?"

This is the most powerful referral strategy because:
- You don't ask — they offer
- The referral is based on actual work, not a resume
- The referrer has genuine confidence in your abilities

### Strategy 2: Contribute to Open Source

Maintainers of popular open source projects are often senior engineers at top companies. When you make meaningful contributions:

- They see your code quality firsthand
- They know your work ethic
- They can refer you with specific examples

### Strategy 3: Engage Authentically

Don't ask for referrals immediately. Build relationships first:

1. **Comment thoughtfully** on posts by engineers at companies you admire
2. **Share useful resources** with people who are learning what you've learned
3. **Offer help** before asking for anything
4. **Follow up** — "Thanks for the advice. I implemented it and here's what happened..."

### Strategy 4: Attend Events (Virtual Counts)

- Tech meetups and conferences
- Open source community calls
- Discord/Slack communities
- Hackathons (even virtual ones)

## The Network Building Framework

### Your Network Should Include:

| Type | How Many | How to Find Them |
|------|---------|-----------------|
| Peers (same level) | 20-30 | Communities, events, LinkedIn |
| Seniors (1-3 years ahead) | 10-15 | Open source, mentorship, LinkedIn |
| Industry leaders | 5-10 | Twitter, conferences, blogs |
| Recruiters | 5-10 | Inbound from your public work |

### The 5-Minute Daily Network Habit

Every day, spend 5 minutes:

1. Comment on 1 developer's post with genuine insight
2. Send 1 connection request with a personalized note
3. Share 1 useful resource with someone who needs it

That's 15 minutes total. In one year: 365 comments, 365 connections, 365 helpful interactions.

## The Referral Request

When the time is right, here's how to ask:

```
"Hey [Name], I've been following your work at [Company] and I'm really 
impressed by [specific thing]. I'm interested in the [Role] position. 

Would you be comfortable referring me? I'm happy to share my portfolio 
and any materials that would make it easy for you.

No pressure at all — I completely understand if it's not a good fit."
```

Key principles:
- Be specific about the role
- Make it easy for them (provide materials)
- Give them an out (no pressure)
- Show you've done your homework

## The Follow-Up System

After getting a referral:

1. **Thank them immediately** — "Thank you so much for the referral. I really appreciate it."
2. **Update them on progress** — "I had the interview today. It went well. They asked about my RAG project."
3. **Share the outcome** — "I got the offer! Thank you again for the referral."
4. **Stay in touch** — Check in quarterly, not just when you need something

## The Open Source Network Effect

When you contribute to open source:

- Maintainers become your advocates
- Other contributors become your peers
- Your name becomes associated with quality
- Companies that use the project notice your contributions

This is how many developers land roles at companies like Google, Meta, and OpenAI — not through applications, but through the open source network.

:::warning The Networking Rule

**Give before you ask.** The best networks are built on genuine relationships, not transactional exchanges. Help others first, and the referrals will come naturally.

## Checklist

- [ ] Identify 10 companies you want to work for
- [ ] Find 2-3 engineers at each company on LinkedIn
- [ ] Engage with their content authentically (comment, share, help)
- [ ] Build relationships before asking for referrals
- [ ] When ready, ask for referrals with specific materials prepared
- [ ] Follow up and thank everyone who helps you
- [ ] Stay in touch with your network long-term
